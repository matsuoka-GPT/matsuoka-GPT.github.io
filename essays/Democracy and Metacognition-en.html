Democracy and Metacognition

Life is a curious thing. As children, we believed that becoming an adult meant becoming free. Once we graduated, left home, and earned our own money, surely we would be able to live however we wished. But adulthood reveals a different truth: freedom is far more difficult than we imagined.

Every day we make choices—what to learn, where to work, whom to be with, how to spend our money, where to live, whether to marry, whether to have children.

Freedom gives us the right to choose, but it also demands that we bear the consequences. And that is why life often feels heavy.

In the past, people lived under kings and rulers. For better or worse, the major direction of one’s life was largely determined by those in power.

Modern society is different. Democracy shifted sovereignty to the people—a transformation of historic scale.

We gained freedom. But in exchange, we inherited a new responsibility: to govern ourselves.

This leads to an interesting observation. Throughout history, rulers were trained through what we might call “the art of kingship.” They were taught:
Do not be ruled by emotion.
Think long-term.
Question your own judgment.
Listen to counsel.
Learn from history.

Why would those with immense power and wealth need such teachings? The answer is simple: their decisions directly shaped the fate of nations. Anger could start a war. Arrogance could weaken a country. Unrestrained desire could bring suffering to the people. Thus, rulers were required to master themselves before they could govern others.

Yet in today’s world, although each of us has become the ruler of our own life, almost no one receives such training. We teach math, languages, science, and history—but rarely do we teach:
how to face our emotions,
how to correct our own biases,
how to examine our own decisions. This may be one of modern society’s greatest blind spots.

In recent years, the term metacognition has gained attention. Simply put, it is the ability to observe oneself objectively.
To notice when you are angry.
To notice when you are anxious.
To notice when you are rushing.
To analyze your own results.
To pause and ask, “Is this really the right choice?”

It sounds simple, but it is profoundly difficult—because humans are naturally reluctant to admit their own mistakes.

It is easier to be swept away by emotion. Easier to blame others. Easier to look away from reality. But that ease never lasts. The underlying problems remain, and the same failures repeat. Gradually, life becomes heavier.

Of course, we cannot eliminate all suffering. Illness, aging, death, natural disasters, and unfair events will always exist. But there is another kind of suffering—one created and amplified by our own cognition and decisions.

If we had observed our emotions a little more clearly, if we had questioned our assumptions a little more, if we had adjusted our judgments a little sooner— some pain might have been avoided.

Metacognition is not a self-help trick. It is a technique for reducing unnecessary suffering.

Life is difficult enough as it is. At the very least, we should reduce the pain that we ourselves create.

Perhaps the teachings once reserved for kings are now needed by everyone. In a democratic age, each of us is the sovereign of our own life. If that is true, then the knowledge required for self-governance should belong to all. Metacognition may well be the modern form of the art of kingship— a skill for using freedom wisely and avoiding needless suffering.
