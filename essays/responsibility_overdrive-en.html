When Responsibility Spins Out of Control

People often say that heroes go astray because of “lust for power” or “selfish ambition.” But when we look at the phenomenon through the lens of structural thinking, a different landscape appears. When a hero steps off the right path, what drives them is not egoistic desire— but an overwhelming sense of responsibility.

Let’s frame this through the story of a company president. By a string of early luck, he managed to build his business. He protected his employees’ livelihoods, treated clients with sincerity, arrived earlier than anyone else, and left later than everyone. He was what society calls “a capable person.” Naturally, people began to trust him—and eventually entrusted him with everything.

“If it’s the president, it’ll be fine.”
“We’ll follow your judgment.”
“Please make the decision for us.”

At first, he felt proud. But the more he was entrusted with, the heavier the weight on his shoulders became— like snow piling up silently, relentlessly.

One year, the company faced a severe crisis. Cash flow tightened, payments to suppliers were at risk, and even employee salaries were uncertain. Banks were cold, and clients were unforgiving.

At that moment, he thought:

“If I can just get through this, we can recover.”
“If I endure this alone, no one else will be hurt.”
“This is a lie told to protect everyone.”

The first act of window-dressing was a small numerical adjustment. No one noticed. No one was harmed. And the company was saved—at least for the moment.

But fortune had already abandoned him. The next month, the situation worsened again. He altered the numbers once more. Then again, to cover the previous lie. And again.

Before he knew it, a logic had taken root in his mind: “If it’s for the company’s survival, my lies are justified.”

This was not greed. It was a compulsive sense of responsibility— the belief that “If I stop, everyone will suffer.”

But responsibility can narrow one’s vision. He could no longer see how cornered he had become. People continued to treat him as a hero, and no one noticed his growing isolation.

And then one day, the deception reached its limit.

The company collapsed. He bore all the blame. Employees lost their jobs, clients were furious, and society labeled him a villain.

But those who truly knew him said:

“He only wanted to protect everyone.”

A hero’s downfall is not the death cry of someone drunk on power. It is the tragedy of a person whose sense of responsibility exceeded human limits, warping their judgment in the loneliness of leadership.

And this structure can emerge in any era, any society, any organization.

Blaming the hero is easy. But the real question is: Why did everyone allow one person to carry everything alone? That is the deeper structure of collective psychology we must confront.

Of course, this does not excuse the consequences. But applying this structural lens to figures in history may be a meaningful thought experiment. Why did we entrust everything to just one person…?
