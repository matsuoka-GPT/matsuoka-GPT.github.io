Is Living in Space Rational?

On a summer night, while looking up at the sky, there are moments when a simple question quietly appears: does humanity really need to live in space? The humid air drifts slowly, and distant stars flicker faintly in the dark. In that stillness, the grand story of space settlement suddenly begins to be measured against the scale of reality. The romance is beautiful, but what does it look like structurally? That wandering thought rises slowly under the summer sky.

Cities in space, humans living on Mars, colonies on the Moon—such futures are often described as if they were almost inevitable in films, news, and technology narratives. Yet when viewed structurally, their rationality is almost nonexistent. It may be that the romance contained in the phrase “space migration” merely covers over the harshness of reality. That is how it begins to feel.

What comes to mind first is the story of “land shortage” that was often told during Japan’s period of rapid economic growth. At the time, people seriously said that the population would grow too large and that there would eventually be nowhere left to live. Today, however, the population is declining, vacant houses are increasing, and cities are beginning to shrink. That old anxiety about land shortage was a social story created to fill uncertainty about the future. Space settlement has a very similar structure. Phrases such as “Earth will become too small,” “resources will run out,” and “we will need a new frontier” arise from the human tendency to solve future anxiety through expansion.

But the actual environment of space is far harsher than even the most extreme places on Earth. Mars has an atmospheric pressure of only about 0.6% of Earth’s, an average temperature around −60°C, radiation levels far higher than those on Earth, toxic soil, and no breathable oxygen. Its gravity is only about one-third of Earth’s, so long-term residence would rapidly reduce bone density and even alter the shape of the heart. The Moon is harsher still: daytime temperatures near 120°C, nighttime temperatures around −170°C, no atmosphere at all, and dust as sharp as glass fragments. If inhaled, it could be fatal.

If humanity had the technology to live comfortably in such environments, then settlements in the Arctic, Antarctic, deep ocean, or underground cities should already have become normal. The polar regions are cold, but they have oxygen. The deep sea is dark, but radiation is low. Underground spaces are enclosed, but pressure is stable. Compared with Mars or the Moon, all of these are almost heavenly environments. And yet humanity does not live there at scale.

In other words, the assumption that space settlement is feasible does not align well with the realities we can already observe on Earth.

Moreover, in an era of population decline, the rationality of a “new frontier” disappears. As in Japan and many other advanced societies, the world population is likely to peak and eventually move toward decline. In a shrinking-population era, shortages of resources, land, and living space become weaker pressures. Cities contract, space becomes available, and redistribution within Earth becomes overwhelmingly more rational. Structurally speaking, the need to seek a new home in space may simply vanish.

So why does space settlement continue to be discussed? Because its value lies less in rationality than in story. Nations can display technological power. Private companies can offer investors a dream. Media can dramatize a romantic future, and science-fictional hope can capture the public imagination. Space settlement functions not as structural rationality, but as a socially useful narrative.

The realistic use of space will probably remain limited to research stations and resource extraction. A lunar base may be valuable as an observation site, and helium-3 mining could someday become relevant to energy. Mars may host robotic laboratories. Orbital space stations have clear value as places for scientific experiments. But as an expansion of the human living base, the rationality is almost absent. The return is too small for the cost.

The thought that came to me while looking at the summer night sky settles, in the end, into this conclusion: space settlement is beautiful as romance, but it has almost no structural rationality. The future humanity should move toward may not lie beyond the stars, but in the redistribution and redesign of life within the planet called Earth.
