Why Does Body Odor Occur?
— The Myth of Cleanliness and the History Written by Our Diet —

1. Introduction

Why is body odor so often treated as a reflection of a person's character?

Although it is usually discussed as something unpleasant, it is also strangely associated with cleanliness, lifestyle, or even personality.

But is body odor really a matter of effort or personal discipline?

When viewed from a structural perspective, the answer lies somewhere quite different.

2. Sweat Is Not the Real Cause

Sweat is not the source of body odor—it is the messenger.

Many people assume that sweating itself creates odor. In reality, fresh sweat has very little smell.

Body odor develops when compounds released through the skin interact with the microorganisms naturally living on its surface.

In other words, sweat simply carries information from inside the body to the outside world.

3. Where Do the Odor-Producing Compounds Come From?

The answer begins with what enters the body.

Many substances found in sweat and skin oils originate from nutrients that have been digested, absorbed, and metabolized.

Dietary fats, proteins, spices, and processed foods all become part of the body's metabolic processes before some of their byproducts are eventually released through the skin.

In that sense, body odor can be viewed as one expression of our dietary history appearing with a delay.

4. Diet Influences the Smell of Living Creatures

This principle is not unique to humans.

Farmed fish raised on feed containing citrus ingredients often develop a milder odor.

Pigs fed acorns are known for producing meat with a cleaner aroma.

Herbivores generally have different body odors from carnivores.

These differences are not simply matters of hygiene. They reflect differences in what each animal consumes.

Humans are unlikely to be completely separate from this broader biological pattern.

5. Why Does the Odor Return Even After Washing?

Treating the symptoms does not change the source.

When people become concerned about body odor, they usually begin with external solutions—strong soaps, deodorants, or fragrances.

These products can certainly provide temporary relief.

Yet the odor often returns because the underlying processes inside the body remain unchanged.

If the input stays the same, the output is likely to remain similar.

6. Why Can Dietary Changes Affect Body Odor?

The goal is not simply to hide odor, but to reduce the conditions that produce it.

Many odor-related compounds are fat-soluble, remain in the body for relatively long periods, and take time to be broken down.

By contrast, many compounds found in fruits and vegetables are water-soluble and are generally processed more quickly.

Improving dietary habits therefore changes the body's internal environment rather than merely masking external symptoms.

7. Body Odor Is Not a Matter of Willpower

It is largely a matter of biological design.

It is easy to explain body odor by saying someone lacks cleanliness, discipline, or simply has "bad genetics."

Such explanations overlook the larger biological system.

Body odor is better understood as the outcome of many interacting factors—including what enters the body, how it is metabolized, and how it is processed by the skin.

8. Conclusion

For people concerned about body odor, the most rational approach is not simply to wash more frequently or add stronger fragrances.

Looking at the body's internal inputs—especially long-term dietary habits—may be just as important as managing the external symptoms.

Body odor is not simply a measure of cleanliness or personal effort.

It is one visible outcome of complex biological processes occurring beneath the surface.

Understanding that structure allows us to replace blame with understanding—and to seek solutions that address causes rather than appearances.
