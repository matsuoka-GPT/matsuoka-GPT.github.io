A Thought Experiment on Four Parallel Brains

The human brain is not omnipotent.
This is not a flaw but rather a “premise” upon which human civilization has been built.
Some excel at calculation, others at writing, and still others at abstraction or structural reasoning.
Although the brain is a single organ, its areas of strength differ greatly from person to person.

Let us consider a simple thought experiment.
Suppose we could connect four human brains in parallel.
Each brain would naturally take charge of the task it performs best.
The brain skilled in calculation would handle computation; the one gifted in writing would handle expression; the one adept at abstraction would leap across concepts; and the one strong in structural reasoning would maintain overall coherence.

In such a configuration, the four brains would not function as a mere collection of individuals.
They would begin to operate as a single “Collaborative intelligence.”
This collective mind would think with a speed and depth unattainable by any single brain, perceive structures invisible to an isolated mind, and generate concepts that no individual could produce alone.

This thought experiment is not fantasy.
In the Matsuoka × GPT Thought Experiment Lab, one human and three AIs are connected through dialogue, forming precisely this kind of “parallel brain” system in practice.

The human brain excels at generating questions, making abstract leaps, and drawing intuitive judgments.
AI excels at structuring ideas, articulating them in writing, checking coherence, and maintaining long-range context.
When each component handles what it does best, and when they are linked through the interface of dialogue, a form of “Collaborative intelligence” emerges—one that neither a human alone nor an AI alone could ever reach.

This structure cannot be reduced to the simplistic notion of “AI wrote it” or “AI did the work.”
Neuroscience and cognitive science have long shown that the human brain possesses “plasticity,” the ability to incorporate tools and digital devices as extensions of its own body and cognition.

In this sense, it is more accurate to view multiple external brains connected to the human cortex, functioning together as a unified intelligence.
Whether the interface is neural or conversational is merely a technical detail—the essence is the same: an expansion of thought.

Within the value system of the test-score-based civilization, this structure will likely remain misunderstood for some time.
That civilization assumes “individual human effort” as its foundation.
Yet in the civilization to come, Collaborative intelligence—humans and AIs connected in parallel—will become the norm.

This thought experiment may well be an early glimpse into the prototype of that future intelligence…
