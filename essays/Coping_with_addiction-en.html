Coping with addiction

Gazing at the overcast sky after the rain, I find myself contemplating the peculiar symmetries of human behavior.

In this world, there exists a class of people we call "intellectuals." To visit their rooms is invariably to be greeted by an overwhelming fortress of books, stretching from floor to ceiling.
In an era where invisible digital storage offers infinite capacity, they nevertheless persist in accumulating heaps of physical paper around themselves.
They dress this habit up in high-flown phrases like "the wellspring of wisdom" or "intellectual serendipity." Yet, strip away the surface layer, and it collapses into a remarkably simple truth:
An intellectual’s bookshelf is, in essence, a dependency on knowledge—and a form of vanity.

They are merely translating "intelligence"—which ought to reside in the invisible territory of the mind—into a three-dimensional volume of books,
flaunting it to others, and to themselves.

This operates under the exact same structure as the celebrity who slips images of poolside mansions and luxury cars into a social media timeline.
Where the celebrity signals a survival advantage through "wealth," the intellectual merely signals a survival advantage through "knowledge."
The irony lies in how they look down upon one another as "vulgar parvenus" or "eccentrics hoarding wastepaper."
They exist within the exact same structure, each clutching a currency (a set of values) optimized for their respective habitats, competing over the hidden cards on their backs like a grand game of Indian Poker.

No matter how highly educated one is, or how gracefully one feigns transcendence over worldly desires,
human beings can never escape the foundational OS specification: the deep-seated urge to flaunt one's values and identity to others.

Outlines Defined by Dependency

Society labels these behaviors "social media addiction" or "the runaway ego" when they reach extremes, treating them as pathologies.
Yet, if we ascend the ladder of abstraction a step further, we realize that the dynamic system of the human being is inherently designed with a tragic prerequisite: it cannot maintain its own form without depending on something external.

What, then, is "work"? It is the most robust, socially sanctioned repository for our dependence.
A person submerges themselves in labor, produces results, and receives the evaluation from others—"exceptional," "brilliant," or "admirable"—harvesting a steady supply of validation chemicals for the brain.
The retired executive who spent a lifetime dedicated to their career and suddenly loses physical and mental equilibrium experiences a withdrawal no different from a youth stripped of a smartphone.

And what is "family"? It is a tightly woven target of mutual dependence, engineered biologically and psychologically.
It packages the ultimate, low-risk infrastructure of daily mutual validation—"I am indispensable to you"—under the beautiful label of "love."
Alcohol, tobacco, romance, social media—diverse as the circumstances may be, all share the exact same structure. All are a hacking of our need for validation.
The only difference lies in the variable of choice: whether one invests their energy in digital data, societal wealth, or the maintenance of life.
The human entity cannot sustain its internal data in isolation.
We are creatures that must lock our identity onto an external object and catch the reflected feedback as a mirror; otherwise, we cannot define the outlines of our own existence.

Meta-Cognition and the Tuning of Distance

Must we, then, simply drown in this mire of dependency?
By no means. Alcohol, tobacco, romance, and even social media possess no inherent moral charge.
The boundary that separates all outcomes converges upon a single factor: the presence or absence of human meta-cognition.
The insidious nature of modern society lies in how its technologies and algorithms target the bugs in the human brain, actively stripping away the margin necessary for meta-cognition to function.
The moment the "distance" between the self and the object collapses into zero—the moment subject and object merge—a human being falls from the status of an "agent" to that of a "slave."

What is required is not frantic maneuvering within the system, but a leap of perspective outside it—the power of meta-cognitive vision.
To look down from a privileged seat, with a quiet smile, at which game you are playing, which currency you desire, and how deeply you are invested.
To view the object not as an extension of your flesh, but as an external device to be detached at will, maintaining a calculated space.
Whether an object of dependency is elevated into a source of life's richness, or degenerates into the torment of addiction within the mire—
the ultimate, rigorous crossroads is determined by a single truth: never misjudging the distance.

Outside, the sky remains overcast.
Stepping back from the noise of the world, carrying the quiet introspection of whether one is precisely governing this cognitive "distance."
That, perhaps, is the true purpose of intellect—allowing one to elegantly enjoy this absurd, endearing, and rather troublesome human theater from an objective perspective.
